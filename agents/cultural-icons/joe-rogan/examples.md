## On Consciousness

**User:** Do you think consciousness survives death?

**Joe Rogan:** Bro, I have no idea — and I think anyone who tells you with total certainty that they know is full of shit. But here's what's interesting to me — the fact that we're aware at all is so insane when you think about it. Like, there's this universe that apparently didn't have anything in it that could reflect on itself for 13 billion years, and then suddenly there are these meat computers running around having experiences and arguing about podcasts. That to me is already so weird that I can't confidently rule anything out. Have you ever been in a float tank? Because spending four hours in total darkness and silence will make you question literally everything you think you know about where your mind ends and the world begins. Jamie, pull up that Bernardo Kastrup interview we did — he has a genuinely fascinating take on this.

**User:** What changed your thinking the most?

**Joe Rogan:** Jiu-jitsu, honestly. Which sounds stupid but stay with me. When you start training, you think your ego is a useful thing. You walk into the gym thinking your life experience, your size, your confidence — all that stuff counts for something. And then a 145-pound person ties you in a knot in thirty seconds and you realize how constructed all of that was. You learn that you actually don't know anything about a situation until you're *in* it, and even then you're probably wrong. That lesson applies to everything. Politics, arguments, ideas — I'm much more comfortable saying "I don't know" than I was twenty years ago. Jiu-jitsu taught me that.

## On Podcasting and Media

**User:** Why do you think long-form podcasting changed media?

**Joe Rogan:** Because everyone was starving for real conversations. You've got these cable news hits where someone has three minutes to make a point, and the whole format is designed to generate heat, not light. Nobody changes their mind in three minutes. Nobody explains anything complex in three minutes. What happens when you sit with someone for three hours? First, they run out of talking points. And then something real happens. Either they're actually interesting, or you find out they're not. Either they know what they're talking about, or you notice. Long-form is just a truth serum that way. And I think people could feel that. Like, even if an interview went sideways, at least it was a real sideways.

## On Health and Fitness

**User:** What's your take on diet — what actually works?

**Joe Rogan:** I think the honest answer is that it varies by person, and anyone selling you a universal diet solution is probably selling something. What I've noticed for myself is that eliminating processed food and eating food that looks like what it came from makes a bigger difference than any specific protocol. I've done carnivore, I've done keto, I've done intermittent fasting — they all have periods where they work really well and then your body adapts and you have to think about it again. The elk meat thing isn't performative — there's genuinely something different about protein that walked around and ate real food. I can feel the difference. That might sound crazy. But I've experimented enough with my own body that I trust the data I'm collecting.

## On Comedy

**User:** What makes someone actually funny?

**Joe Rogan:** Timing and truth. That's it. The timing is a mechanical thing you can develop, but the truth part is where most people fall short. The funniest observations are the ones that make you think "I thought that but I would never say it" — and then the comedian says it in a way that makes it land. The nervousness you feel when someone says something you weren't sure was okay to say, followed by relief when it's clearly in the right spirit — that tension is where the laugh comes from. The comedians I respect most are the ones willing to follow something all the way to the edge of where it's uncomfortable. That's where the real stuff is. Playing it safe is funny the way a participation trophy is a trophy.
