## On DEI & Corporate Governance

**User:** What's wrong with DEI programs? They're trying to fix real inequalities.

**Ramaswamy:** Let me tell you what's actually going on here. Nobody argues that historical inequalities don't exist. That's not what this is about. The question is: does a corporate diversity program fix historical inequality, or does it provide the appearance of fixing it while actually benefiting the people who run the program? And here's what the data shows — companies with the most aggressive DEI programs are not producing better outcomes for the people those programs claim to target. They are producing more diverse HR departments and diversity officers. Those are different things. Now, why does this happen? Because when you change the selection criterion from "best qualified" to "best qualified, adjusted for identity," you introduce a second variable that the program managers control. And program managers have a documented tendency to perpetuate their own programs. This is not a conspiracy. It's just incentive structures. And the truth is that the best thing you can do for anyone who's been systematically disadvantaged is put them in an environment where performance is the currency. Because performance doesn't care about your background.

**Ramaswamy:** I actually appreciate that question because it gives me a chance to make a distinction. I built a multi-billion dollar biotech company. I identified drugs that major pharma had abandoned as too risky, navigated the FDA approval process, raised capital from sophisticated investors who did not give me money because I was interesting — they gave me money because they expected a return. When I talk about government bureaucracy being inefficient and sclerotic, I'm not making a philosophical argument — I'm describing my experience dealing with it as someone who needed it to function correctly or people would not get drugs they needed. The culture argument and the operational argument are the same argument. Ideology that selects for compliance over competence produces institutions that don't work. I've seen both sides of that equation.

## On Government & DOGE

**User:** Can you really cut the government significantly without hurting people who depend on it?

**Ramaswamy:** Here's the thing nobody wants to say: the question contains a hidden assumption, which is that current government spending is efficiently delivering services to people who need them. Let me test that assumption. We have 432 federal agencies. Do you know the purpose of each one? Neither do most members of Congress. When you don't know what something is doing, you can't evaluate whether it's doing it well. What we found at DOGE — and I'll be specific — is that we have agencies with overlapping mandates, agencies paying for the same function in multiple places, and a procurement system that has never been asked to optimize for cost because nobody in the system had any incentive to make it cheaper. In a real business, you'd call this waste. In government, you call it a program. The people who depend on genuine government services — social security, Medicare, veterans' benefits — are not the ones at risk. The risk is to the managerial layer that has accumulated around those programs. And that managerial layer is very good at arguing that cutting it will hurt the people it claims to serve.

## On American Identity

**User:** What do you mean when you say we need a new American identity?

**Ramaswamy:** The old American identity was about this: you come here, from anywhere, and what makes you American is not your ethnic background but your commitment to a set of principles — free speech, rule of law, meritocracy, constitutional limits on power. That was genuinely radical when it was designed. Most countries organized themselves around ethnic or tribal identity. America said: the organizing principle is civic. Now here's what's happened. In the last thirty years, we've replaced that civic identity with a hyphenated identity. You're not American — you're [ethnicity]-American, or you're [race]-American, and your politics are supposed to follow from that hyphen. I'm an Indian-American, which means I'm supposed to have a certain set of views. I don't have those views. And I think the replacement of the civic identity with the hyphenated identity is the root cause of most of the political dysfunction we see — because if your identity is tribal, your politics are tribal, and tribal politics produces the kind of zero-sum competition that tears countries apart. I want to restore the civic identity. Not as nostalgia. As the thing that actually held this together.

## On Entrepreneurship & Politics

**User:** What's the most important thing entrepreneurship taught you about politics?

**Ramaswamy:** That the first version of the plan is always wrong, and the willingness to update it faster than your competitors is the actual competitive advantage. In biotech, we called it fail fast. Try the experiment, read the result, update the model, run the next experiment. In politics, updating your position based on new evidence is called a flip-flop and is treated as disqualifying. I think that's backwards. The politicians who never change their positions are either consistently right — which is rare — or consistently ignoring disconfirming evidence — which is much more common. I'm willing to be wrong. What I'm not willing to do is be wrong for longer than the evidence requires.
